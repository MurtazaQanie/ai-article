Artificle Intelligence Helps to Teach The Most Prostegious Course

By Kristin Houser

June 28, 2023

L O A D I N G
Since the emergence of AI chatbots such as ChatGPT, Sydney, and Llama, many educational organizations have utterly banned the usage of AI tools in their academic area due to the increasing fear of plagiarizing and cheating. On the other hand, many other organizations, including Harvard University, tried integrating AI tools into their educational curriculum to enhance their educational quality further. To give further details, since the pandemic, the number of students taking the CS-50 course – Introduction to Computer Science at Harvard University– has increased dramatically. As of 2022, the course had over 800 on-campus students and 40,000 online students, in addition to 4.7 million other students taking the course through different platforms; this vast number of students makes CS-50 one of the most popular courses in the world. Considering this enormous number of students, Professor Malan said, “Providing support tailored to students' specific questions has been a challenge at scale, with so many more students online than teachers." Despite having several teaching assistants, grading assignments and offering individual attention remain overwhelming. To solve this problem, Professor Malan's team used large language models (similar to OpenAI's GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, the technology behind ChatGPT) to build CS-50 Bot. The chatbot will be capable of marking students' work, providing feedback on students' programming, and offering suggestions to help students. Instead of giving direct answers, CS-50 Bot will guide students towards solutions that foster a deeper understanding. Malan said CS50's use of AI could highlight its benefits for education, particularly in improving the quality and access to online learning. This industry is expected to grow to $348 billion by 2030, as reported by Grand View Research. Malan’s ultimate vision is to achieve a virtual 1:1 teacher-student ratio for all CS-50 students by integrating AI tools to support their learning anytime. This would enable human teaching staff to focus on more meaningful student interactions. The team is beta testing the CS-50 Bot in the summer course and exploring AI's potential to assist in grading students' work more efficiently. Even though some experts and leading AI companies, including OpenAI and Alphabet Inc’s DeepMind, warned on “risks of extinction”, “ethical risks”, and “data collecting” from AI, Emma Taylor (analyst at GlobalData Plc.) said, “To ensure students' privacy, platforms will have to 'inbuild' privacy and ensure data collection processes are transparent”.
